\begin{abstract}
The gut microbiome modulates host neurotransmitter production through metabolic pathways involving serotonin, $\gamma$-aminobutyric acid (GABA), and dopamine precursors, yet the higher-order structural organization of microbial communities that supports this function remains uncharacterized. Here we apply persistent homology from topological data analysis (TDA) to bacterial co-occurrence networks derived from 16S rRNA and metagenomic sequencing data across three cohorts (Human Microbiome Project, American Gut Project, and curatedMetagenomicData). We construct Betti curves and persistence landscapes as vectorized topological descriptors of microbial community shape and identify persistent loops and voids in the co-occurrence landscape that distinguish neurotransmitter-producing configurations from depleted states. We show that fungal (mycobiome) co-colonization and antimicrobial resistance gene carriers act as topological disruptors, fragmenting the connected components and collapsing the loop structures required for cross-feeding networks that sustain tryptophan metabolism and GABA biosynthesis. Sliding-window analysis reveals abrupt topological regime transitions that precede compositional shifts in neurotransmitter-associated taxa. These topological biomarkers correlate with intestinal permeability markers, short-chain fatty acid profiles, and self-reported neurological and mood outcomes, providing a structural framework linking microbial network topology to the gut-brain axis. Our results suggest that optimizing community topology---not merely taxonomic composition---may be a prerequisite for microbiome-based strategies targeting neurotransmitter production.
\end{abstract}
