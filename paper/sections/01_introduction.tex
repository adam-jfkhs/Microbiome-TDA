\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}
%=============================================================================

The human gut microbiome comprises trillions of microbial cells whose
collective metabolic activity influences host immunity, energy balance, and,
increasingly, inflammatory disease severity~\citep{hmp2012structure,
turnbaugh2007human}.
Standard characterization tools---alpha and beta diversity metrics, relative
abundance profiles---treat the microbiome as a bag of taxa: they count who is
present and in what proportion, but they do not describe \emph{how} those taxa
relate to one another.
Co-occurrence networks partially address this limitation by encoding pairwise
correlation structure, yet summaries of co-occurrence networks (node count,
edge density, modularity) remain insensitive to the higher-order topology
of the interaction landscape---the loops, voids, and multi-scale connectivity
patterns that may carry distinct biological signal.

\subsection{The structure problem in microbiome analysis}

Microbial communities are not collections of independent actors.
Taxa interact through cross-feeding, competition, niche exclusion, and
quorum sensing, forming an ecological network with emergent structural
properties~\citep{faust2012microbial,friedman2012inferring}.
When this network is disrupted---by antibiotic treatment, by inflammatory
bowel disease (\ibd), by dietary shifts---the disruption may manifest not
primarily in the loss of particular species but in the reorganization of
interaction topology.
Two communities could have identical species richness and similar pairwise
correlations yet differ fundamentally in whether those correlations form
cycles, dense cliques, or disconnected modules.

Persistent homology, a central tool in topological data analysis (\tda),
provides a mathematically rigorous way to quantify this higher-order
structure~\citep{carlsson2009topology, edelsbrunner2008persistent}.
Applied to a co-occurrence network, it extracts the number and
``lifetime'' (persistence across correlation thresholds) of connected
components (\Hzero), one-dimensional loops (\Hone), and higher-order
cavities.
These topological descriptors have been applied successfully in
neuroscience~\citep{ghrist2008barcodes}, protein structure analysis, and
materials science~\citep{wasserman2018topological,bubenik2015statistical},
but their application to gut microbiome co-occurrence networks remains
nascent~\citep{camara2017topological}.

\subsection{IBD as a structural disruption model}

Inflammatory bowel disease---comprising Crohn's disease and ulcerative colitis
(UC)---is characterized by chronic intestinal inflammation, barrier
dysfunction, and a consistently altered microbiome~\citep{petersen2017defining}.
Metagenomic and 16S rRNA studies have documented reductions in microbial
diversity, depletion of butyrate-producing taxa (\textit{Faecalibacterium
prausnitzii}, \textit{Roseburia} spp.), and expansion of
pro-inflammatory Proteobacteria~\citep{ley2006microbial}.
Yet these compositional findings do not fully explain the clinical
heterogeneity of \ibd.
We hypothesize that \ibd\ disrupts the topological organization of microbial
co-occurrence networks in a way that is detectable and robust, and that this
topological disruption represents a complementary dimension of dysbiosis
beyond compositional shifts.

\subsection{Our contribution}

We make the following specific contributions:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*, itemsep=2pt]
  \item \textbf{Methodological:} We introduce a paired bootstrap design for
  comparing persistence diagrams between groups, using sign-flip label
  permutation as the null and a globally fixed taxon set to ensure every
  iteration operates on a common feature space (Section~\ref{sec:methods}).

  \item \textbf{Empirical:} We apply this design to 3,409 AGP stool samples
  across three biologically motivated comparisons: \ibd\ status, antibiotic
  recency, and plant-based diet. We find that \ibd\ is associated with
  dramatically lower topological complexity across all six \Hone\ features
  ($d = 0.75$--$2.38$; Section~\ref{sec:results}).

  \item \textbf{Robustness:} We demonstrate that the \ibd\ finding is fully
  preserved after covariate matching on age, sex, and BMI, and is consistent
  across taxon-set sizes $N \in \{50, 80, 120\}$
  (Section~\ref{subsec:sensitivity}).

  \item \textbf{Statistical transparency:} We pre-specify and apply a single,
  explicit FDR correction policy (18-test BH, sign-flip primary $p$-values
  only; Section~\ref{subsec:stats}) and report all 36 test results
  (full and matched subsets) without selection.
\end{enumerate}

The remainder of this paper is organized as follows.
Section~\ref{sec:methods} describes the dataset, preprocessing pipeline,
network construction, \tda\ approach, and statistical framework.
Section~\ref{sec:results} presents results for each comparison,
followed by the sensitivity analysis.
Section~\ref{sec:discussion} interprets findings in the context of \ibd\
biology and discusses limitations.
Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} concludes with implications and directions for
future work.
