\subsection{Dataset and group characteristics}
\label{subsec:dataset_chars}

After filtering to stool samples with $\geq 1{,}000$ reads and taxa
present in $\geq 5\%$ of samples, 3,409 samples remained (1,864 taxa before
global top-80 selection).
Table~\ref{tab:groups} summarizes group sizes for each comparison.
The IBD group ($n = 165$) was smaller than the antibiotics and diet groups,
reflecting the population prevalence of inflammatory bowel disease in the
\agp\ cohort.
Among the 201 metadata columns, fields for antibiotic recency
(\texttt{ANTIBIOTIC\_SELECT}), IBD status (\texttt{IBD}), and diet type
(\texttt{DIET\_TYPE}) were used as group definitions.

\subsection{IBD is associated with reduced topological complexity}
\label{subsec:ibd_results}

\Ibd-associated microbiomes (UC + Crohn's) showed consistently and strongly
\emph{reduced} topological complexity compared to healthy controls across all
six \Hone\ features (Figure~\ref{fig:ibd_bootstrap}).

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{../figures/agp_ibd_v2.png}
  \caption{%
    \textbf{Paired bootstrap distributions: IBD vs.\ Healthy.}
    Each panel shows the distribution of $\delta = f^{(A)} - f^{(B)}$ across
    200 paired iterations for one topological feature
    (group A = IBD; group B = Healthy).
    Red histograms: full groups; green histograms: age/sex/BMI-matched subset.
    The dashed vertical line marks $\delta = 0$.
    All six distributions are shifted to the left of zero, indicating that
    IBD microbiomes have fewer and shorter-lived co-occurrence loops.
    Effect sizes (Cohen's $d$), permutation $p$-values, and confirmatory
    Wilcoxon $p$-values are shown in each panel header; all six features are
    FDR-significant (BH, 18-test correction; Table~\ref{tab:ibd_results}).
  }
  \label{fig:ibd_bootstrap}
\end{figure}

Table~\ref{tab:ibd_results} reports the full statistical results for the IBD
comparison.
The largest effect was observed for total \Hone\ persistence
($d = -2.28$, mean 0.40 vs.\ 0.80 for IBD vs.\ healthy at $N = 80$) and
max Betti-1 ($d = -2.07$, mean 3.4 vs.\ 7.1).
H1 count showed $d = -1.34$ (mean 16.9 vs.\ 23.7), indicating that IBD
microbiomes form approximately 7 fewer co-occurrence loops per subsample than
healthy controls.

an explanation for the observed differences.

\subsection{Plant-based diet is associated with altered loop structure}
\label{subsec:diet_results}

Plant-based diet (vegan, vegetarian, and pescatarian; $n = 367$) was
compared to omnivore ($n = 2{,}747$).
Figure~\ref{fig:diet_bootstrap} shows the bootstrap delta distributions.

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{../figures/agp_diet_v2.png}
  \caption{%
    \textbf{Paired bootstrap distributions: Plant-based vs.\ Omnivore.}
    Red = full groups; green = matched subset.
    Plant-based eaters show longer-lived H1 features (positive $\delta$ for
    mean and max lifetime) and higher max Betti-1, indicating denser and
    more persistent co-occurrence loops.
    H1 count and entropy show a negative delta (plant-based communities have
    \emph{fewer} but \emph{longer-lived} loops), suggesting a structural
    reorganization rather than uniform enrichment.
  }
  \label{fig:diet_bootstrap}
\end{figure}

Five of six features were FDR-significant in the full-group analysis.
Notably, mean lifetime ($d = 0.65$; $p_\text{FDR} = 0.004$) and max
Betti-1 ($d = 0.46$; $p_\text{FDR} = 0.004$) showed robust positive effects,
while H1 count was negative ($d = -0.34$), indicating that plant-based
microbiomes harbor \emph{fewer but longer-lived} co-occurrence loops.
In matched analyses (308 vs.\ 922), three of six features survived FDR
correction (mean lifetime, max lifetime, max Betti-1), suggesting that
the diet signal is partly confounded by demographic differences but not
entirely explained by them.

\subsection{Antibiotic recency shows limited and variable signal}
\label{subsec:abx_results}

Antibiotic use within the past 6 months ($n = 590$) was compared to no
antibiotic use in the past year ($n = 2{,}271$).
In full-group analyses, only max Betti-1 reached FDR significance
($d = -0.39$; $p_\text{FDR} = 0.004$).
In matched analyses (479 vs.\ 1{,}284), four of six features were FDR-significant
at $N = 50$ and $N = 120$ but not at $N = 80$, indicating that the antibiotic
signal is sensitive to taxa-set choice and should not be reported as robust.
We interpret this as a weak and potentially confounded effect; antibiotic
recency within 6 months spans a wide range of time-since-treatment and
antibiotic classes, which may dilute any topological signal.

\subsection{Taxa-set sensitivity: IBD is robust; diet and antibiotics are not}
\label{subsec:sensitivity}

Figure~\ref{fig:sensitivity} shows Cohen's $d$ values and FDR significance
across all three comparisons and three taxon-set sizes ($N \in \{50, 80,
120\}$) for full groups.

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{../figures/taxa_sensitivity_full.png}
  \caption{%
    \textbf{Taxa-set sensitivity: Cohen's $d$ by $N$ taxa (full groups).}
    Heatmap cells show Cohen's $d$ for each feature (rows) and taxon-set
    size (columns) within each comparison (panels).
    Cells marked $\star$ are FDR-significant (BH correction across 18 tests,
    sign-flip permutation $p$-values).
    \textit{Centre panel (IBD):} All 18 cells are strongly negative and
    FDR-significant, demonstrating complete robustness to taxon-set size.
    Effect sizes range from $d = -0.75$ to $d = -2.38$.
    \textit{Right panel (Diet):} Partial robustness; 3--4 of 6 features are
    FDR-significant across sizes.
    \textit{Left panel (ABX):} Variable signal; 1--3 of 6 features
    FDR-significant, with inconsistent direction.
  }
  \label{fig:sensitivity}
\end{figure}

The matched-group sensitivity heatmap (Supplementary Figure~S1) shows an
identical pattern for IBD: all 18 cells remain FDR-significant with effect
sizes $d = -0.81$ to $d = -2.56$.
